## üåæ Project Overview  

This project is a **video-based data story** examining how the rapid adoption of Artificial Intelligence in agriculture affects **food affordability**, especially in the years following COVID-19.  

Instead of relying solely on charts or static visuals, this project uses **scrollytelling**, animation, and narrative sequencing to show how food prices shifted:

- *before COVID*  
- *during global supply chain disruptions*  
- *after the accelerated adoption of AI-driven tools in agriculture*  

It pushes the viewer to consider an important question:  

### **If AI makes production more efficient, why didn‚Äôt food become more affordable?**

---

## ü§ñ Why AI? Why Food Affordability?

During COVID-19:

- labour shortages  
- transportation delays  
- supply chain instability  
- rising production costs  

forced agricultural systems to adopt new technologies ‚Äî including AI ‚Äî much faster than expected.

AI promised:

- efficiency,  
- automation,  
- lower losses,  
- and faster supply movement.  

But at the consumer level, **prices stayed high**, raising questions about equity, access, and who truly benefits from technological advancements.

---

## üé¨ Why a Video Format?

Some stories need movement, pacing, and emotional buildup.  
A video format allowed me to:

- create **smooth narrative transitions**,  
- contrast pre- and post-COVID realities,  
- embed **storytelling and emotion**,  
- and visually mirror the real-life experience of watching prices climb over time.  

This format turns analysis into a **story people can feel**, not just observe.

---

## üìä Data & Analytical Approach  

The analysis behind this story involved:

- exploring monthly retail food price data  
- identifying pre- vs post-COVID inflation patterns  
- examining AI-related agricultural interventions  
- comparing AI adoption timelines with price volatility  
- mapping affordability trends across food categories  

The notebook (`FoodAffordability.ipynb`) contains the detailed exploratory analysis.

The raw dataset (`MonthlyRetailPrices.xls`) is also included in this folder for transparency and reproducibility.
